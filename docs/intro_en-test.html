Yamato Tea

A traditional cup nurtured by the nature and history of Nara.

What is Yamato Tea?

Grown mainly in the Yamato Highlands of Nara Prefecture, traditional Yamato Tea is characterized by its high aroma and a refreshing, clear taste despite its astringency.
Each cup is a blend of Nara's rich nature and its timeless history.

History and Climate

The Origin of Yamato Tea

The history of Yamato Tea dates back to 806 A.D. in the early Heian period. It is said to have begun when Kobo Daishi Kukai brought tea seeds back from Tang China and sowed them at Butsuryu-ji Temple (now in Uda City).

Introducing the Production Area (Taharu District, Nara City)

Climate and Soil of Taharu District

The main production area, the Yamato Highlands, is known for its cool climate at an altitude of 200-500 meters. The significant temperature difference between day and night encourages the tea leaves to grow slowly, creating an ideal environment for a rich aroma and deep flavor.

The Taharu district, in particular, which has been known as a Yamato Tea production area since ancient times, is where this blessed environment is most concentrated.

Meet the Producers

We'd like to introduce you to the passionate producers in the Taharu district who are carrying on the tradition of Yamato Tea.

Taharu Natural Farm

Sawa Fukui

After working at a department store, Sawa decided to pursue a career in agriculture, wanting to work with nature. She grows tea and vegetables without any pesticides or chemical fertilizers. Based on her belief that "people, tea, vegetables, and insects are all connected by yui (bonds)," she practices tea farming that values harmony with nature. Her tea is characterized by a refreshing sweetness and a clean astringency.

Resource-Recycling Agriculture / No Pesticides, Chemical Fertilizers, or Herbicides

The Farmer of Kubota Apple Orchard Who Makes Organic Matcha

Hiroshi Kubota

Matcha made by a tea farmer whose family has been in the business for over 100 years.
After retiring at age 60, Mr. Kubota converted a part of his tea field into an apple orchard. The Taharu district, where the farm is located, is blessed with significant temperature differences between morning and night and abundant water sources, making it ideal for tea cultivation. By carefully grinding tea leaves grown only with organic compost using a stone mill, he produces matcha with a rich aroma, depth, and sweetness. It can be enjoyed on its own or used as an ingredient for sweets and baking.

Organic Compost / Matcha

Matcha Stone Grinding

Stone grinding is a traditional method of pulverizing the raw material for matcha, "tencha," using a stone mill just before shipping. The stone mill consists of an upper and lower stone with intricate grooves. By placing tencha in the upper stone and turning the handle, the tea leaves are slowly ground.

While mixers and other machines can produce matcha quickly with their high-speed rotation, they tend to create uneven particles, and the friction heat can damage the aroma and flavor.

In contrast, the time-consuming and laborious stone grinding process generates little friction heat, resulting in a uniform and highly aromatic matcha.

Exploring the Flavor Profiles

Yamato Tea reveals diverse characteristics depending on the production method.
Let's compare their unique profiles with a bar chart.

Sencha

Known for its refreshing aroma, balanced umami, and moderate bitterness. It's widely enjoyed as a daily tea.

Kabusecha

The tea leaves are covered with a black cheesecloth before harvest, which suppresses astringency and brings out a mellow umami.

Fukamushicha (Deep-steamed Tea)

Steamed for longer than usual, it has a rich flavor and a deep green color. It is characterized by low astringency and a smooth, easy-to-drink profile.

Matcha

A fine powder of green tea leaves carefully ground with a stone mill. It's popular not only for tea ceremonies but also for a wide range of uses, including sweets and cooking.

The Origin of Yamato Tea

The Roots of Yamatocha

Yamato Tea traces its roots back to the early Heian period, when the revered monk Kobo Daishi Kukai brought tea seeds from China and planted them at Butsuryuji Temple (present-day Uda City). Since then, Yamato Tea has symbolized quality and tradition.

Ideal Growing Conditions

The Yamato Highlands

The Yamato Highlands, at 200–500 meters above sea level, offer a cool climate with significant temperature differences between day and night. This allows tea leaves to grow slowly, developing rich aroma and deep flavor.

The Tawara District, a historic tea-producing area, embodies this perfect environment.

Meet Our Passionate Tea Growers

With every sip, you can feel the richness of nature and over 1,200 years of history.

Sawa Fukui – Tawara Natural Farm

After working in a department store, Sawa Fukui pursued a life close to nature, growing tea and vegetables without any chemicals. She believes, “People, tea, vegetables, and insects… everything is connected through ‘Yui’ (bonding).” Her teas are known for their refreshing sweetness and subtle astringency.

Hiroshi Kubota – Kubota Apple & Tea Farm

Born into a family of tea farmers with over 100 years of tradition, Hiroshi Kubota produces premium matcha. After retiring at 60, he converted part of his tea fields into apple orchards. Using only organic compost and grinding leaves carefully with traditional stone mills, he creates matcha with a rich aroma and naturally sweet, deep flavor—perfect for sipping or adding to desserts and baked goods.

Authentic Stone-Ground Matcha

Stone grinding is a traditional method that slowly turns tencha leaves into fine powder just before shipment. Unlike machines that produce heat and uneven particles, stone grinding preserves the aroma and creates a smooth, high-quality matcha.
